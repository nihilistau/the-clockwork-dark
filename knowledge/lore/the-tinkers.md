# The Tinkers

The tinkers are the country's nerve-endings: wagon-folk who trade twice a season,
remember every debt and every road, and carry the news no notice board prints.
Where Maris's oven ([[maris-hearth]]) is the still heart of Edgewood, the tinkers
are its restless edge — they have *seen* the wheat stand wrong three valleys over,
and they price old clock parts at twice their weight in copper because they know
what the parts are becoming.

**Ilya of the Nine Pins** is the tinker who comes to Edgewood: androgynous, sharp-
eyed, nine brass pins in the scarf, a slightly unsettling smile. Ilya sells
sympathy charms and ward pins "that sometimes work and sometimes merely reassure,"
and maps of roads that shift when the wheat turns. Ilya is also *ambiguous* by
design — the Assistant's tinker face overlaps so cleanly that nobody, including
the engine's narration, will commit to which is which (see [[the-two-powers]]).
This is the point: help from a tinker is help you cannot quite trust, and that is
exactly the texture the game wants.

Tinkers traffic in the one craft that bites the Dark back: **sympathy and naming**
([[sympathy-and-naming]]). A sympathy lamp, a ward bell, a charm spoken over — these
are tinker work, half real and half reassurance, and the difference matters most
when it is too late to test. Their questlines ([[the-tinkers-questline]]) tend to
end with a real tool pressed into your hand and a quiet request to *hold the line a
while longer*.

The tinkers will not save the country; they are not heroes, only the people who
noticed first and kept moving. But the road-knowledge they carry — and the things
they build from brass filings and old names — are how an ordinary traveler learns
to push back against [[the-clockwork-dark]] at all.
