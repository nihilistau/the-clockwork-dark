# The Drowned Carillon

Beneath the grey shelf of the Brass Coast, in a cathedral the sea took three
hundred years ago, an organ still plays. No hand touches it. The tide presses
through its drowned pipes and the Carillon answers — a low chord you feel in
your fillings before you hear it, rolling inland on the wet wind.

It does not flood or storm. It *tunes*. Each note the Carillon strikes calls a
little more of the sea ashore and a little more of the living out of true:
fishers come home with brass bells set in their throats; gulls ring instead of
cry; a child's name, sung once too often near the water, comes back in the
Carillon's own voice. What it takes, it keeps as resonance — salt, verdigris,
and a chime where a heartbeat used to be.

Its hunger is for **song and grief**: the more a thing is mourned and remembered
in music, the cleaner the Carillon can sound it out and unmake it. This is why
the keepers of [[bellfounders-quay]] have gone silent — no hymns, no dirges, no
lullabies — while the water climbs the steps one tuned note at a time. Its
advance is measured by [[the-receding-tide]]; the quay still stands at the dry
margin, where [[salt-widow-vesh]] mends nets and [[brother-cael]] keeps a bell
he will not ring.
